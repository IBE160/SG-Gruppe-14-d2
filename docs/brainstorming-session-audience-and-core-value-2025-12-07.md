### 3.6 Success Metrics (How We Measure Value Delivery)

#### User Engagement Metrics

**UE-1: Completion Rate**
- **Definition:** % of users who start the simulation and successfully submit a valid plan (within budget and timeline)
- **Target (MVP):** ‚â• 60% completion rate
- **Rationale:** If the simulation is too difficult or frustrating, students will abandon it. 60% allows for challenge while ensuring most students can succeed with effort.
- **Measurement:** Track `games.status = 'completed'` vs. `games.status = 'in_progress'` or `'abandoned'`

**UE-2: Average Time to Completion**
- **Definition:** Mean time from first login to successful plan submission
- **Target (MVP):** 45-60 minutes
- **Rationale:** Aligns with the goal of a focused, time-efficient learning experience. Too fast suggests the simulation is trivial; too slow suggests it's tedious.
- **Measurement:** `games.completed_at - games.created_at` for completed games

**UE-3: Negotiation Iteration Count**
- **Definition:** Average number of messages exchanged with AI suppliers per WBS item before acceptance
- **Target (MVP):** 3-7 messages per supplier
- **Rationale:** Too few messages (1-2) suggests the simulation is too easy or students are accepting bad deals. Too many (>10) suggests the AI is too stubborn or the game is frustrating.
- **Measurement:** `COUNT(chat_logs WHERE wbs_item = X AND game_id = Y) / COUNT(DISTINCT wbs_items WHERE game_id = Y)`

**UE-4: Renegotiation Rate**
- **Definition:** % of users who renegotiate at least one WBS item after initially accepting a quote
- **Target (MVP):** ‚â• 40%
- **Rationale:** Indicates students are experiencing the iterative planning process (realizing later that earlier decisions put them over budget).
- **Measurement:** Track changes to `plan_entries` for the same WBS item within a game

**UE-5: Retry Rate (Failed Plans)**
- **Definition:** % of users who submit a plan that fails validation (exceeds constraints) at least once before succeeding
- **Target (MVP):** 50-70%
- **Rationale:** The simulation should be challenging enough that most students don't get it perfect on the first try, forcing them to make trade-offs and renegotiate.
- **Measurement:** Track plan submissions that fail validation before final success

#### Learning Outcome Metrics

**LO-1: Negotiation Strategy Diversity**
- **Definition:** Variety of negotiation approaches used (e.g., referencing requirements, offering trade-offs, challenging assumptions, proposing alternatives)

## 4. Key Themes & Insights

### Key Themes:

1. **Soft Skills Gap in PM Education:**
   - Traditional PM education focuses heavily on tools (Gantt charts, PERT, CPM) and light on negotiation, persuasion, and data validation
   - The most valuable learning happens in the "messy middle" of planning - the iterative back-and-forth that case studies skip

2. **Safe Experimentation Enables Learning:**
   - Users need permission to fail without consequences
   - The ability to retry, renegotiate, and experiment with different strategies is more valuable than getting it "right" the first time
   - Fear of real-world failure prevents learning through trial-and-error

3. **Authenticity Drives Engagement:**
   - Generic, abstract simulations feel like "just another assignment"
   - Using a real case study (H√¶dda) that students already study creates immediate relevance and context
   - AI personas must feel realistic (not scripted) to transfer learning to real-world situations

4. **Time as a Constraint AND a Feature:**
   - Students and professionals need focused, efficient learning experiences (not 10-hour simulations)
   - But the experience must be meaty enough to create genuine challenges (not a 10-minute quiz)
   - Sweet spot: 45-60 minutes for meaningful struggle followed by achievement

5. **Feedback Loops Accelerate Learning:**
   - Real-time validation (budget tracking, constraint checking) allows users to self-correct
   - Delayed feedback (waiting for exam results) misses the teachable moment
   - The simulation becomes a "learning companion" rather than a "testing mechanism"

6. **Persona-Driven AI Creates Depth:**
   - Different suppliers requiring different negotiation tactics prevents the simulation from feeling repetitive
   - Users must develop adaptive strategies, not just memorize "the right answer"
   - Persona diversity mirrors real-world stakeholder management

7. **Iteration Mirrors Reality:**
   - The best learning happens when users realize their early decisions were wrong (e.g., accepting 110 MNOK for grunnarbeid means they can't afford r√•bygg later)
   - The ability to renegotiate teaches that project plans are living documents
   - This is a lesson that static case studies cannot provide

### Insights & Learnings:

1. **The Simulation Fills a Specific Gap:**
   - It doesn't replace Gantt chart tools (MS Project) - it teaches what comes BEFORE you open MS Project
   - It doesn't replace theory (PMBOK) - it teaches how to apply theory when humans are involved
   - It doesn't replace case analysis - it teaches the messy data-gathering phase that cases skip

2. **Users Have Different Success Definitions:**
   - Students: Passing the course, being able to discuss in class
   - Career switchers: Confidence to take on first PM role, portfolio artifacts
   - Instructors: Student engagement, learning outcomes, ease of integration
   - The MVP must serve the primary user (students) while not alienating secondary users

3. **The Core Value is NOT "Learn Project Management":**
   - The core value is: "Practice the specific skills (negotiation, data validation, trade-offs) that PM courses teach in theory but never let you practice"
   - This is a narrower, more defensible value proposition than "general PM education"

4. **AI Realism is Make-or-Break:**
   - If the AI feels like a chatbot ("I can do anything you want!"), the simulation fails
   - If the AI feels robotic ("I will only accept exactly 105 MNOK"), the simulation feels frustrating
   - The AI must feel like a real human with constraints, motivations, and a negotiation strategy

5. **Success Metrics Must Measure Learning, Not Just Engagement:**
   - Completion rate is important, but not sufficient
   - We need to measure: Did users develop negotiation skills? Did they learn to iterate? Did they use evidence?
   - This requires qualitative analysis (chat logs, negotiation strategies) not just quantitative metrics (time to completion)
